Chapter 04 · Founder Dynamics

Equity and Co-Founder Agreements

Co-founder equity splits are among the most consequential and emotionally fraught negotiations any entrepreneur will face. Get it wrong and you create resentment, misaligned incentives, and legal landmines that can destroy your company from within. This chapter teaches you to structure equity agreements that are fair, motivating, and resilient to the inevitable changes every startup experiences.

Estimated: 55–75 min
6 Key Concepts
3 Assignments

Why This Matters

According to Noam Wasserman's research at Harvard Business School, 65% of high-potential startups fail due to conflict among co-founders, not because of market conditions, competition, or product-market fit. The root of most co-founder conflict is equity — how much each person owns, what happens when circumstances change, and whether the split reflects evolving contributions over time. The equity conversation is the first major negotiation of your startup, and it sets the tone for every negotiation that follows.

Most first-time founders make one of two critical errors. The first is the "equal split by default" — dividing equity evenly among co-founders on day one because the conversation feels awkward and equality seems fair. Research shows that equal splits correlate with lower startup success rates because they signal an inability to have difficult conversations and often fail to reflect genuine differences in contribution, risk, and commitment. The second error is the "handshake deal" — agreeing on equity verbally without a written agreement, vesting schedule, or provisions for what happens when someone leaves. Both errors create ticking time bombs.

This chapter gives you the frameworks, scripts, and legal knowledge to negotiate co-founder equity with confidence. You will learn how to evaluate contributions objectively, structure vesting that protects everyone, draft agreements that anticipate change, and have the hardest conversation in entrepreneurship — telling someone you trust and care about that you think the equity should not be equal.

Critical Rule

Never operate a multi-founder startup without a written co-founder agreement. Verbal agreements are unenforceable, memories diverge over time, and when conflict arises (it will), the absence of a written agreement turns a business dispute into a personal betrayal. Get the agreement done before you write the first line of code or make the first sales call.

Key Concepts

1. Factors That Determine Fair Equity Splits

A fair equity split is not about equality — it is about proportionality. Each co-founder brings different things to the table, and the split should reflect the relative value, risk, and commitment of each person. The challenge is that these factors are subjective, emotionally charged, and often change over time. Using a structured framework removes some of the emotion and creates a shared language for the conversation.

Framework 
/* ── Equity Split Evaluation Framework ────────────────────── */

FACTOR 1: IDEA ORIGINATION (weight: 5-10%)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Who conceived the original idea?
  Important: Ideas alone have minimal value. Execution is everything.
  A reasonable weight for idea origination is 5-10% of total split.
  If the idea has existing IP, patents, or prior work, this increases.

FACTOR 2: DOMAIN EXPERTISE (weight: 10-20%)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Who has irreplaceable knowledge of the market, technology, or customers?
  Consider: Is this expertise rare? Could it be hired for?
  Deep domain expertise that would take years to develop → higher weight
  General business skills that could be hired → lower weight

FACTOR 3: TIME COMMITMENT (weight: 20-30%)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Is everyone going full-time? When does each person start full-time?
  Part-time co-founder vs. full-time co-founder is a major difference.
  Delayed full-time commitment should mean a smaller initial equity stake
  (with the option to earn more through vesting or milestones).

FACTOR 4: FINANCIAL RISK (weight: 10-20%)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Is anyone investing capital? Taking below-market salary? Both?
  Opportunity cost matters: Someone leaving a $400K job takes more risk
  than someone leaving a $80K job (in absolute terms).
  Consider: Can be compensated via salary differential rather than equity.

FACTOR 5: ROLE CRITICALITY (weight: 15-25%)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Who will be CEO? CTO? What does the startup need most right now?
  If it is a technical product, the technical co-founder may warrant more.
  If it is a sales-driven business, the business co-founder may warrant more.
  The CEO role typically carries 5-10% premium for additional responsibility.

FACTOR 6: NETWORK AND RELATIONSHIPS (weight: 5-15%)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Who brings investors, customers, advisors, or key hires?
  Relationships that directly generate revenue or funding have high value.
  General "I know people" has lower value than specific, committed intros.

2. Vesting Structures That Protect Everyone

Vesting is the mechanism that ensures equity is earned over time, not given as a lump sum on day one. Without vesting, a co-founder who leaves after three months walks away with the same equity as someone who stays for five years. Standard vesting is 4 years with a 1-year cliff — meaning no equity vests until the first anniversary, then it vests monthly or quarterly thereafter. Vesting is not punishment; it is insurance that protects all co-founders from early departures.

Vesting 
/* ── Vesting Structures for Co-Founders ───────────────────── */

STANDARD 4-YEAR VESTING WITH 1-YEAR CLIFF
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Month 0-11:  0% vested (cliff period, nothing vests)
  Month 12:    25% vests at once (the "cliff")
  Month 13-48: Remaining 75% vests monthly (1/48th per month)

  Example: Co-founder with 30% equity
  Month 6 departure:   0% vested → leaves with 0%
  Month 12 departure:  25% vested → leaves with 7.5%
  Month 24 departure:  50% vested → leaves with 15.0%
  Month 48 completion: 100% vested → keeps full 30.0%

ACCELERATION TRIGGERS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Single Trigger: All unvested equity vests immediately if the
  company is acquired. Protects founders from being "acqui-hired"
  and then fired, losing unvested equity.

  Double Trigger: Unvested equity vests only if the company is
  acquired AND the founder is terminated or demoted within 12 months.
  More common; investors prefer this because it keeps founders motivated
  through transitions.

REVERSE VESTING FOR FOUNDERS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Founders receive all shares on day one but subject to a repurchase
  right that lapses over the vesting schedule. Economically identical
  to forward vesting but has tax advantages (83(b) election).
  /* Always file 83(b) within 30 days! Missing this is irreversible. */

3. Anatomy of a Co-Founder Agreement

A co-founder agreement is the legal document that governs the relationship between founders. It should be drafted before the company is formally incorporated, or as close to that point as possible. The agreement covers equity, roles, decision-making, departure scenarios, and intellectual property. Every provision exists because of a real situation where founders without that provision ended up in court.

Agreement 
/* ── Essential Co-Founder Agreement Provisions ────────────── */

SECTION 1: EQUITY AND VESTING
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  - Exact equity split for each co-founder
  - Vesting schedule (4 years, 1-year cliff is standard)
  - Acceleration provisions (single or double trigger)
  - What happens to unvested shares if someone leaves

SECTION 2: ROLES AND RESPONSIBILITIES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  - Who is CEO, CTO, COO (and what that means day-to-day)
  - Time commitment expectations (full-time, part-time, hours/week)
  - When each co-founder transitions to full-time
  - How role changes are decided as the company grows

SECTION 3: DECISION-MAKING
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  - What decisions require unanimous consent vs. majority
  - Deadlock resolution mechanism (mediator, advisor vote, etc.)
  - Spending authority limits per co-founder
  - How major pivots or strategy changes are decided

SECTION 4: DEPARTURE PROVISIONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  - Voluntary departure: what happens to vested and unvested equity
  - Termination for cause: definitions and consequences
  - Non-compete and non-solicitation terms
  - Company repurchase rights on vested shares (at FMV or cost basis?)

SECTION 5: INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  - All IP created for the company belongs to the company
  - Prior IP assignments (what each founder brings in)
  - Invention assignment during and after employment
  - Confidentiality obligations

SECTION 6: FINANCIAL PROVISIONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  - Initial capital contributions (if any)
  - Salary structure and when salaries begin
  - Expense reimbursement policies
  - How future fundraising dilution is shared

4. Having the Equity Conversation

The equity conversation is one of the most emotionally difficult discussions you will ever have with someone you care about. It requires you to put a number on the relative value of your respective contributions — which feels like ranking your friendship. The key is to separate the emotional relationship from the business negotiation, use objective frameworks, and approach the conversation as collaborative problem-solving rather than adversarial negotiation.

Script 
/* ── Equity Conversation Script ───────────────────────────── */

OPENING: SET THE FRAME
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "I want to have the equity conversation because I've read that
  startups that avoid this conversation often fail because of it
  later. I want us to be one of the teams that does this right.
  Can we approach this as a collaborative problem we solve together?"

STEP 1: SHARE FRAMEWORKS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "Here are the factors most startup advisors say matter for equity
  splits: [share the evaluation framework]. Let's each independently
  score ourselves and each other on these factors, then compare."

STEP 2: DISCUSS DIFFERENCES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "I see we scored differently on [factor]. Help me understand your
  perspective. What am I missing about your contribution in this area?"

STEP 3: PROPOSE WITH RATIONALE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "Based on our discussion, I think a fair split would be [X/Y/Z]
  because [specific reasoning tied to factors]. What do you think?
  I'm open to adjusting if I'm not seeing something correctly."

STEP 4: ADDRESS VESTING
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "Regardless of the split, I think we should both vest over 4 years
  with a 1-year cliff. This protects both of us — if either of
  us had to leave for any reason, the other person wouldn't be stuck
  with a co-founder who owns a huge stake but isn't contributing."

STEP 5: DOCUMENT AND REVISIT
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "Let's get this written up by a lawyer and plan to revisit in 6
  months once we see how things play out. We can adjust if needed
  before we take outside investment."

5. Dynamic Equity Models

Some founders prefer dynamic equity models that adjust ownership based on actual contributions over time. The most well-known is the "Slicing Pie" model by Mike Moyer, which treats the startup like a poker game where each contribution (time, money, ideas, relationships, equipment) adds chips to the pot, and your ownership equals your chips divided by total chips. This eliminates the need to predict future contributions and ensures fairness based on actual effort.

Model 
/* ── Dynamic Equity: Slicing Pie Model ────────────────────── */

THE CORE FORMULA
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Your Equity % = Your Slices / Total Slices

HOW CONTRIBUTIONS ARE VALUED
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Time:       Hours worked x fair market hourly rate x 2 (risk multiplier)
  Money:      Cash invested x 4 (risk multiplier for early capital)
  Ideas/IP:   Appraised value or negotiated value x 2
  Equipment:  Fair market value x 2
  Connections: Value of actual revenue or funding generated x negotiated rate

  Example:
  Founder A: 500 hours x $150/hr x 2 = 150,000 slices
  Founder B: 300 hours x $200/hr x 2 = 120,000 slices
  Founder B: also invested $50K x 4 =  200,000 slices
  Total slices: 470,000
  Founder A: 150K / 470K = 31.9%
  Founder B: 320K / 470K = 68.1%

WHEN TO USE DYNAMIC EQUITY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Good fit:  Pre-funding, uncertain commitment levels, side projects
  Poor fit:  Post-funding (investors want fixed cap tables),
             teams with clear roles and equal commitment

FREEZE POINT
━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Dynamic equity must convert to fixed equity at some point,
  typically when you: raise outside capital, reach profitability,
  or hit an agreed milestone. After the freeze, standard vesting
  applies to the fixed amounts.

6. Advisor Equity and Early Employee Grants

Equity negotiations extend beyond co-founders to advisors and early employees. Getting these wrong creates either resentment (too little equity for critical early contributors) or excessive dilution (too generous with people who contribute minimally). Understanding market standards gives you the confidence to negotiate these arrangements fairly.

Benchmarks 
/* ── Advisor and Early Employee Equity Benchmarks ─────────── */

ADVISOR EQUITY (2-year vesting, no cliff is standard)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Standard advisor:   0.1% - 0.25% (monthly meetings, introductions)
  Active advisor:     0.25% - 0.5% (weekly engagement, deep involvement)
  Expert advisor:     0.5% - 1.0% (domain-critical, hands-on work)
  Board advisor:      0.5% - 2.0% (attends board meetings, strategic role)

  Warning: Advisors who ask for 1%+ without significant, measurable
  commitment are usually not worth the equity. Get specific deliverables
  in writing before granting advisor shares.

EARLY EMPLOYEE EQUITY (4-year vesting, 1-year cliff)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  Employee #1-5:    0.5% - 2.0% (depending on role and seniority)
  Employee #6-15:   0.1% - 0.5%
  Employee #16-30:  0.05% - 0.2%
  VP-level hire:    0.5% - 1.5% (Series A stage)
  C-level hire:     1.0% - 3.0% (non-founder CTO, CFO, etc.)

  These percentages are of fully-diluted equity at the time of grant.
  They will dilute with future funding rounds.
Pro Tip

Use the FAST Agreement for advisors. The Founder/Advisor Standard Template (FAST) from the Founder Institute is a widely-used, standardized advisor agreement that covers equity, vesting, IP assignment, and confidentiality. Using a standard template signals professionalism and reduces legal costs.

Assignment 1
Level 1 — Foundation

Equity Split Evaluation Exercise

Using the Equity Split Evaluation Framework from this chapter, score a hypothetical three-person founding team and propose a split with rationale for each factor.

  • Create profiles for 3 hypothetical co-founders with different strengths (technical, business, domain expertise)
  • Score each co-founder on all 6 evaluation factors with specific justifications
  • Calculate a weighted equity split based on the scores
  • Design a vesting schedule with cliff and acceleration provisions
  • Write a one-paragraph justification for the proposed split that you could share with the team
Done when

You have a complete scored evaluation, a specific equity split proposal with vesting terms, and a clear written rationale.

Assignment 2
Level 2 — Applied

Co-Founder Agreement Drafting

Draft a complete co-founder agreement covering all six sections outlined in this chapter. Use real or hypothetical terms.

  • Draft equity and vesting provisions including cliff, acceleration, and departure scenarios
  • Define roles, responsibilities, and time commitment expectations for each co-founder
  • Create decision-making rules including deadlock resolution and spending authority
  • Draft departure provisions covering voluntary exit, termination for cause, and equity repurchase
  • Include IP assignment and confidentiality clauses
Done when

You have a multi-page co-founder agreement draft that covers all six sections with specific, actionable provisions.

Assignment 3
Level 3 — Strategic

Equity Negotiation Role-Play

Simulate the equity conversation with a partner. One person advocates for a 60/40 split, the other for 50/50. Use the frameworks and scripts from this chapter to reach an agreement.

  • Create confidential role briefs: one co-founder has more domain expertise but less time commitment, the other has full-time availability and capital
  • Conduct a 30-minute equity conversation using the script framework from this chapter
  • Record the final agreed split and all provisions (vesting, roles, departure terms)
  • Debrief: What tactics worked? What felt uncomfortable? Where did the framework help?
  • Switch roles and repeat. Compare how the conversation dynamics changed
Done when

You have completed two equity conversations from both sides, documented the outcomes, and reflected on which negotiation approaches were most effective.

Self-Check

Before moving to Chapter 05, test your understanding of co-founder equity dynamics.

Can you name at least 5 factors that should determine a co-founder equity split?

The six key factors are: (1) idea origination, (2) domain expertise, (3) time commitment, (4) financial risk and capital contribution, (5) role criticality (CEO, CTO, etc.), and (6) network and relationships. Each should be weighted based on the specific startup's needs. Ideas alone are worth only 5-10%; execution-related factors carry the most weight.

Why is a 50/50 equity split often a warning sign?

A 50/50 split signals that the founders could not have the difficult conversation about relative contributions. Research by Noam Wasserman shows equal splits correlate with lower success rates. It also creates governance problems because no one has a tiebreaking vote. A split that reflects genuine differences in contribution shows the founders can have hard conversations — a critical skill for building a company together.

What is a vesting cliff and why does it matter?

A vesting cliff is a minimum period (typically 12 months) during which no equity vests. If a co-founder leaves before the cliff, they receive nothing. After the cliff, the accumulated equity vests at once (typically 25%), then the remainder vests monthly. The cliff protects all co-founders from someone who joins, realizes the startup is not for them, and leaves early with a significant equity stake.

When should dynamic equity models be used vs. fixed splits?

Dynamic equity works best pre-funding when commitment levels are uncertain, co-founders are part-time, or contributions are hard to predict. It should convert to a fixed split (freeze point) when taking outside investment, reaching profitability, or hitting an agreed milestone. Post-funding, investors require a fixed cap table and dynamic models become impractical.

What are the essential sections of a co-founder agreement?

The six essential sections are: (1) equity and vesting terms, (2) roles and responsibilities with time commitments, (3) decision-making rules and deadlock resolution, (4) departure provisions covering voluntary exit and termination, (5) intellectual property assignment and confidentiality, and (6) financial provisions including salary and capital contributions.

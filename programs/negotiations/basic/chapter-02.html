Chapter 02 · Phase 1: Foundations

Preparation and Research

The negotiation is won or lost before you sit down at the table. Learn to set clear objectives, research your counterpart, map stakeholder interests, and define your walk-away point — the preparation ritual that separates amateurs from professionals.

Estimated: 50–65 min
6 Key Concepts
3 Assignments

Why This Matters

Studies consistently show that negotiators who spend at least twice as much time preparing as they do negotiating achieve significantly better outcomes. Yet most people walk into negotiations having done little more than decide what they want. Preparation is the single highest-leverage activity in the entire negotiation process.

Preparation transforms negotiation from an improvised confrontation into a structured strategic exercise. When you know your BATNA, understand your counterpart's constraints, and have mapped the zone of possible agreement, you negotiate from strength rather than anxiety. You recognize concessions that cost you little but matter enormously to the other side. You avoid the trap of anchoring on your opening position and forgetting why you came to the table in the first place.

Key Insight

Every minute spent in preparation saves approximately ten minutes of wasted effort at the table. Unprepared negotiators make avoidable concessions, miss creative trade-offs, and often leave value unclaimed that both sides would have happily shared.

This chapter gives you a systematic preparation framework that applies to any negotiation — from asking for a raise to closing a vendor contract to resolving a team conflict. By the end, you will have a repeatable process that becomes second nature.

Key Concepts

1. Setting Clear Negotiation Objectives

Before any negotiation, you need three clearly defined positions: your target (what you ideally want), your reservation point (the worst deal you will accept), and your opening (where you start the conversation). Without these, you are negotiating blind.

framework 
/* ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
   NEGOTIATION OBJECTIVES PLANNING TEMPLATE
   Complete before every significant negotiation
   ═══════════════════════════════════════════════ */

NEGOTIATION: [Name/Description]
DATE:         [Target Date]
COUNTERPART: [Name / Organization]

── OBJECTIVES ───────────────────────────────────

1. PRIMARY OBJECTIVE
   Target (Best Case):       [Specific, measurable outcome]
   Reservation Point:        [Walk-away threshold]
   Opening Position:         [Where you start]
   Justification:            [Why this range is reasonable]

2. SECONDARY OBJECTIVES
   Priority A:               [Important but tradeable]
   Priority B:               [Nice to have]
   Priority C:               [Low cost to concede]

3. RELATIONSHIP OBJECTIVES
   Post-negotiation goal:    [How you want the relationship to look after]
   Trust-building actions:   [Specific gestures planned]

── SUCCESS CRITERIA ─────────────────────────────

   Excellent outcome:        [Define what "great" looks like]
   Acceptable outcome:       [Define what "good enough" looks like]
   Walk-away trigger:        [Define the specific moment you leave]

The most common mistake in objective setting is focusing only on one dimension. A salary negotiation is not just about the number — it includes title, equity, start date, remote flexibility, signing bonus, and professional development budget. List every variable before you prioritize.

2. BATNA Analysis

Your BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) is the most important concept in negotiation theory, introduced by Roger Fisher and William Ury in Getting to Yes. It is not what you hope to get from this negotiation — it is what you will do if you walk away with no deal at all.

Common BATNA Mistakes

Your BATNA is not your wish list. If you are negotiating a job offer, your BATNA is not "a better offer from Google" unless you actually have that offer in hand. A weak BATNA honestly assessed is far more useful than a fantasy BATNA. Overestimating your BATNA leads to walking away from good deals. Underestimating it leads to accepting bad ones.

framework 
/* ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
   BATNA ANALYSIS WORKSHEET
   Assess both your own and counterpart's alternatives
   ═══════════════════════════════════════════════ */

── YOUR BATNA ───────────────────────────────────

Alternative 1:  [Description]
  Feasibility:   [1-10]
  Value:         [Quantify if possible]
  Timeline:      [How quickly can you execute?]
  Downsides:     [What do you lose?]

Alternative 2:  [Description]
  Feasibility:   [1-10]
  Value:         [Quantify if possible]
  Timeline:      [How quickly can you execute?]
  Downsides:     [What do you lose?]

Alternative 3:  [Description]
  Feasibility:   [1-10]
  Value:         [Quantify if possible]
  Timeline:      [How quickly can you execute?]
  Downsides:     [What do you lose?]

BEST BATNA:     [Which alternative is strongest?]

── COUNTERPART'S BATNA ──────────────────────────

Their Alternative 1:  [What they do if no deal with you]
  Your Estimate:        [How attractive is this for them? 1-10]

Their Alternative 2:  [Second option]
  Your Estimate:        [1-10]

THEIR BEST BATNA:    [Your assessment]

── POWER ANALYSIS ───────────────────────────────

Who needs this deal more?  [You / Them / Equal]
BATNA improvement plan:   [Actions to strengthen your position]
Timeline pressure:        [Who has more time?]

The power in any negotiation flows toward the party with the better BATNA. This is why improving your BATNA before a negotiation is often more valuable than improving your negotiation technique. If you are negotiating a job offer, having two competing offers fundamentally changes the dynamic, regardless of your persuasion skills.

3. Stakeholder Mapping

In most real-world negotiations, the person across the table is not the only decision-maker. They report to a boss, consult with colleagues, and face constraints you may not see. Stakeholder mapping helps you understand the full decision-making ecosystem.

  Disposition:  Neutral

  Interests:    Technical compatibility, support quality
  Concerns:     Integration complexity, team bandwidth
  Influence:    High
  Disposition:  Supportive
  Strategy:     Provide technical proof-of-concept, architecture docs

Stakeholder 3: Legal Counsel (Blocker potential)
  Interests:    Compliance, liability protection
  Concerns:     Data privacy, indemnification clauses
  Influence:    Medium
  Disposition:  Neutral to Opposed
  Strategy:     Pre-address compliance questions, offer redlines early

4. ZOPA (Zone of Possible Agreement)

The ZOPA is the range between your reservation point and the other party's reservation point. If your reservation price for selling a car is $15,000 and the buyer's maximum is $18,000, the ZOPA is $15,000 to $18,000. Any deal within that range is better for both parties than no deal at all. 

  YOUR PERSPECTIVE
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  Worst         Reservation                   Target    Opening
  (walk away)   Point                                   Position
                $15K                          $19K      $22K
                │◄─────── YOUR RANGE ────────►│

                     ┌───────────────────┐
                     │       ZOPA        │
                     │  $15K ─── $18K    │
                     │  (Deals happen    │
                     │   in this zone)   │
                     └───────────────────┘

                          $12K       $18K
  THEIR PERSPECTIVE       │◄── THEIR RANGE ──►│
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  Opening       Target              Reservation    Worst
  Position                          Point          (walk away)
  $12K          $14K                $18K
Figure 2: ZOPA visualization — the overlap where deals are possible

When there is no ZOPA — your minimum is higher than their maximum — no amount of negotiation skill will produce a deal. Recognizing this early saves everyone time. The preparation work you do estimating the other side's reservation point determines whether you even approach the table.

Pro Tip

The ZOPA is not fixed. You can expand it by introducing new variables. If the buyer's maximum cash offer is $18K but they could also provide deferred payment, trade a service, or bundle another item, the effective ZOPA grows. This is why multi-issue negotiations almost always create more value than single-issue haggling.

5. Counterpart Research

The more you know about the person across the table, the better you negotiate. This is not about manipulation — it is about understanding their constraints, pressures, and priorities so you can propose solutions that genuinely work for both sides.

/* ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
   COUNTERPART RESEARCH CHECKLIST
   Gather intelligence before the negotiation
   ═══════════════════════════════════════════════ */

── PROFESSIONAL BACKGROUND ──────────────────────

[ ] Current role and tenure
[ ] Previous roles and career trajectory
[ ] Decision-making authority level
[ ] Key achievements and priorities
[ ] Published articles, talks, or interviews

── ORGANIZATIONAL CONTEXT ───────────────────────

[ ] Company financial health (public filings, funding rounds)
[ ] Recent company news (layoffs, expansions, pivots)
[ ] Competitive landscape and market pressures
[ ] Budget cycles and fiscal year timing
[ ] Previous deals they have done (terms, patterns)

── NEGOTIATION STYLE ────────────────────────────

[ ] Reputation among peers (ask your network)
[ ] Preferred communication style (data-driven? relationship-first?)
[ ] Past negotiation outcomes with others
[ ] Cultural considerations
[ ] Known pressure points or sensitivities

── CONSTRAINTS AND PRESSURES ────────────────────

[ ] Deadlines they face
[ ] Internal stakeholders they must satisfy
[ ] Budget limitations or approval processes
[ ] Regulatory or legal requirements
[ ] Relationship with competitors / alternatives

6. The Complete Preparation Checklist

Before every negotiation, run through this comprehensive checklist. It synthesizes all the frameworks above into a single pre-negotiation routine. Print it, tape it to your wall, and never skip it.

framework 
/* ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
   PRE-NEGOTIATION FLIGHT CHECK
   Complete every item before sitting down
   ═══════════════════════════════════════════════ */

── PHASE 1: SELF-ASSESSMENT ─────────────────────

[ ] What do I want? (Target, reservation, opening)
[ ] What is my BATNA? (Honestly assessed)
[ ] What are my priorities? (Ranked 1 to N)
[ ] What can I concede cheaply? (Low cost, high value to them)
[ ] What are my emotional triggers? (What makes me reactive?)

── PHASE 2: COUNTERPART ANALYSIS ────────────────

[ ] What do they want? (Best guess at their objectives)
[ ] What is their BATNA? (What happens if they walk away?)
[ ] What are their constraints? (Budget, time, authority)
[ ] Who influences their decision? (Stakeholder map)
[ ] What pressures are they under? (Deadlines, competition)

── PHASE 3: DEAL DESIGN ─────────────────────────

[ ] What is the likely ZOPA? (Overlap of reservation points)
[ ] What creative options exist? (Expanding the pie)
[ ] What trades can I propose? (My low-cost, their high-value)
[ ] What precedents support my position? (Market data, comps)
[ ] What is my first offer strategy? (Anchor high/low? Let them go first?)

── PHASE 4: LOGISTICS ───────────────────────────

[ ] Venue and setting (Home turf? Neutral ground?)
[ ] Agenda (Who controls the order of issues?)
[ ] Time limits (How long? Multiple sessions?)
[ ] Documentation (Who drafts? What format?)
[ ] Backup plan (What if they surprise me?)
How much preparation is enough?

The answer depends on the stakes. For a routine vendor negotiation, 30 minutes of structured preparation may suffice. For a career-defining salary negotiation, plan for 2–4 hours spread across multiple days. For a major partnership deal, preparation may take weeks and involve a team. The rule of thumb: if you cannot clearly state your BATNA, the other side's likely BATNA, and the estimated ZOPA, you are not ready.

What if I cannot research my counterpart?

Sometimes you negotiate with strangers and have limited information. In that case, focus on what you can control: your own BATNA, your objectives, and your walk-away point. During the opening phase of the negotiation, use active listening (Chapter 03) to gather the information you could not find in advance. Ask open-ended questions about their priorities and constraints. The first 15 minutes of any negotiation should be an information-gathering conversation, not a positional battle.

When should I improve my BATNA before negotiating?

Always. If you have time before the negotiation, use it to strengthen your alternatives. Applying to additional jobs before a salary negotiation, getting quotes from competing vendors before a procurement discussion, or developing an internal solution before negotiating with an external provider all improve your position. The best negotiators spend more time improving their BATNA than rehearsing their arguments.

Scenario Workshop

Apply the preparation framework to this realistic scenario. Work through each step before looking at the analysis.

scenario 
/* ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
   SCENARIO: Software License Renewal
   ═══════════════════════════════════════════════ */

SITUATION:
You are the IT Director at a mid-size company (400 employees).
Your enterprise CRM license is up for renewal in 60 days.
Current cost: $120,000/year for 400 seats.
The vendor's renewal quote: $156,000/year (30% increase).
They cite "market adjustment" and new features added.

YOUR CONTEXT:
- Your team has built significant customizations on this platform
- Migration to a competitor would take 4-6 months
- Your CEO has asked for 10% cost reductions across all software
- Three team members are trained and certified on this platform
- You have budget authority up to $140,000 without CFO approval

THE VENDOR'S CONTEXT:
- They recently lost a major enterprise client to a competitor
- Their fiscal year ends in 45 days
- Your company was featured in their case study last year
- The account manager has been promoted and wants to show retention
- They launched a new tier with AI features (which you don't need)

── YOUR TASK ────────────────────────────────────

1. Define your objectives (target, reservation, opening)
2. Assess your BATNA and theirs
3. Map the stakeholders on both sides
4. Estimate the ZOPA
5. Identify creative options beyond price
Sample Analysis: Objectives

Target: Renew at $110,000/year (8% reduction from current) with a 3-year lock on pricing. Reservation point: $135,000/year (still under CFO threshold and meets the 10% reduction goal if you negotiate down other software costs). Opening: $95,000/year, citing the CEO's cost-reduction mandate and competitor pricing.

Secondary objectives: Extended payment terms (quarterly instead of annual), inclusion of 50 additional seats at no cost for growth, and a dedicated support engineer for migration to their new platform version.

Sample Analysis: BATNA Assessment

Your BATNA: Migrate to Competitor X at $90,000/year, but with 4–6 months of migration cost (estimated $80,000 in staff time and consulting) and productivity loss. Effective first-year cost: $170,000. Your BATNA is weaker than it appears — acknowledge this privately but do not reveal it.

Their BATNA: Lose your account entirely and absorb the revenue hit near fiscal year-end. They also lose a case study reference. Their BATNA is weak, especially with the timing pressure. This gives you more leverage than the raw numbers suggest.

Sample Analysis: ZOPA Estimate

Your reservation: $135,000. Their likely reservation: $105,000–$110,000 (they would rather retain at a discount than lose the account before fiscal year-end). Estimated ZOPA: $105,000–$135,000. A deal at $115,000–$120,000 with additional concessions (extended terms, extra seats) would likely be achievable and satisfactory to both sides.

Assignments

Complete these in order. Each builds on the preparation skills covered above. Document your work in a preparation journal that you will reference throughout the program.

Assignment 1
Level 1 — Foundation

Personal BATNA Assessment

Choose a real upcoming negotiation in your life (job offer, vendor contract, salary review, or even a personal purchase) and complete a full BATNA analysis.

  • Identify at least 3 genuine alternatives to a negotiated agreement
  • Rate each alternative on feasibility, value, and timeline
  • Identify your single best BATNA with honest assessment
  • Estimate the counterpart's BATNA with supporting evidence
  • Write a one-paragraph power analysis comparing both BATNAs
Done when

A peer can read your BATNA analysis and accurately predict your negotiation strategy without you explaining it verbally.

Assignment 2
Level 2 — Professional

Complete Stakeholder Map and ZOPA Analysis

Using the same negotiation from Assignment 1, create a full stakeholder map and estimate the ZOPA.

  • Map all stakeholders on both sides (decision makers, influencers, blockers, gatekeepers)
  • Rate each stakeholder's influence level and current disposition
  • Define engagement strategy for each key stakeholder
  • Draw a ZOPA diagram with estimated reservation points for both sides
  • Identify at least 3 variables that could expand the ZOPA
Done when

Your stakeholder map identifies at least one person you had not previously considered, and your ZOPA analysis reveals at least one creative option for expanding the deal space.

Assignment 3
Level 3 — Mastery

Full Pre-Negotiation Brief

Create a comprehensive negotiation preparation document that could serve as a briefing for a colleague stepping in on your behalf.

  • Complete the full Pre-Negotiation Flight Check template for a real scenario
  • Include counterpart research with at least 5 data points per stakeholder
  • Prepare three contingency plans for different counterpart opening moves
  • Write a one-page executive summary a colleague could use to negotiate on your behalf
  • Have a peer review your brief and identify at least one blind spot
Done when

A colleague who has never seen the negotiation context can read your brief and conduct the first 30 minutes of the negotiation competently without additional coaching.

Self-Check

Before moving to Chapter 03, honestly answer these questions.

Can I articulate my BATNA in one sentence?

If you cannot say "My best alternative is X, which gives me Y" in one clear sentence, you have not completed your BATNA analysis. Go back and distill it. Complexity in your notes is fine — but you need to be able to state it simply under pressure.

Do I know the difference between my target and my reservation point?

Your target is aspirational. Your reservation point is the boundary line. If you find yourself unable to distinguish them, it usually means you have not honestly assessed what you would accept versus what you hope for. Wishful thinking is the enemy of clear reservation points.

Have I identified at least one stakeholder I was not initially aware of?

Good stakeholder mapping always reveals surprises. If your map matches your initial assumptions perfectly, you probably have not dug deep enough. Ask: who has veto power? Who controls the budget? Who would be embarrassed if this deal goes wrong? Those questions often reveal hidden stakeholders.

Can I draw the ZOPA for my next negotiation from memory?

The ZOPA diagram should be burned into your mind before you sit down. You should be able to sketch it on a napkin at a moment's notice. If you cannot, revisit the estimation exercise. The ZOPA is your mental map for the entire negotiation — every offer and counteroffer should be placed on it in real time.

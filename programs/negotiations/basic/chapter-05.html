Chapter 05 · Negotiation Foundations

Salary and Compensation

Whether you are negotiating your own salary or structuring compensation for your startup's first hires, this chapter gives you the data, frameworks, and scripts to handle the most personal negotiation in business.

Estimated: 45–60 min
6 Key Concepts
3 Assignments

Why This Matters

Salary negotiation is the single most financially impactful skill most professionals never learn. Research from Carnegie Mellon shows that people who negotiate their starting salary earn an average of $1 million more over their career than those who accept the first offer. Yet only 37% of workers negotiate their salary, and in startups, where equity adds layers of complexity, the gap between prepared and unprepared negotiators is even wider.

For startup founders, compensation negotiation cuts both ways. You need to attract top talent in a market where FAANG companies offer $300K+ packages, while managing burn rate and equity dilution. Getting compensation right for your first 10 hires sets the trajectory for your entire compensation philosophy. Getting it wrong creates resentment, turnover, and culture problems that compound for years.

Whether you are on the candidate side or the employer side, the principles are the same: preparation beats improvisation, data beats guesswork, and creative deal-making beats positional bargaining. This chapter equips you for both sides of the table with frameworks, scripts, and real-world scenarios.

Key Insight

Compensation is not just salary. It is base pay + equity + bonus + benefits + growth trajectory + work flexibility. The best negotiators expand the pie by trading across these dimensions. A candidate who focuses only on base salary leaves significant value on the table.

Key Concepts

1. Market Research: Know Your Numbers

Before any compensation conversation, you need data. Without market benchmarks, you are negotiating blind — and the other side almost certainly has more information than you do. The asymmetry of information is the single biggest disadvantage candidates face in salary negotiations.

Building a compensation research dossier is not optional — it is the foundation of every subsequent move you make. Spend at least 2-3 hours on research before any compensation conversation. The ROI on this time is extraordinary: even a modest improvement of $5,000 in base salary compounds to over $100,000 across a decade, before accounting for raises built on top of that higher base.

  Glassdoor     — Salary reports + company reviews

  [ ] Base salary range for your role/level/location

  [ ] Equity norms for startup stage (seed vs Series A vs B)
  [ ] Benefits comparison (health, 401k match, PTO)
  [ ] Remote vs. on-site pay differentials
  [ ] Signing bonus ranges for your market

STARTUP EQUITY BENCHMARKS (approximate):
  Employee #1-5:    1.0% - 2.5%
  Employee #6-15:   0.25% - 1.0%
  Employee #16-30:  0.1% - 0.5%

  // These vary enormously. Always check current data.
Pro Tip

When asking peers about their compensation, lead with vulnerability: share your own range first. "I'm making $145K and wondering if that's market rate. Would you be comfortable sharing your range?" This norm-breaking honesty opens doors that indirect questions never will.

2. Total Compensation Framework

Smart negotiators think about total compensation, not just base salary. A $150K base with 0.5% equity at a fast-growing startup can be worth far more than a $180K base with no upside. But equity is risky, and you need frameworks to evaluate the trade-off.

The key insight is that different components of compensation carry different levels of risk, tax treatment, and liquidity. Base salary is guaranteed and liquid. Equity is speculative and illiquid. Bonuses fall somewhere in between. Your job is to weight each component appropriately based on your personal financial situation and risk tolerance.

Framework
/* ── Total Compensation Evaluation Template ───────────────── */

COMPONENT BREAKDOWN:

1. BASE SALARY
   Guaranteed cash. Your floor for financial planning.
   Negotiate this first — everything else is built on it.
   Risk level: NONE (guaranteed)

2. EQUITY (Stock Options or RSUs)
   Potential upside. Evaluate using:
   - Number of shares / total shares outstanding = your %
   - Latest 409A valuation x your shares = current paper value
   - Apply a risk discount (50-80% for early-stage)
   - Vesting schedule (standard: 4 years, 1 year cliff)
   Risk level: HIGH (may be worth $0 or 10x)

3. ANNUAL BONUS / PERFORMANCE BONUS
   Often 10-20% of base at startups. Ask:
   - Is it guaranteed or discretionary?
   - What triggers it? Revenue? OKRs? Manager discretion?
   Risk level: MEDIUM (depends on structure)

4. SIGNING BONUS
   One-time cash to offset what you leave behind.
   Common range: $10K - $50K (higher for senior roles)
   Often negotiable even when base salary isn't.
   Risk level: NONE (one-time guaranteed)

5. BENEFITS & PERKS
   Health insurance quality (deductible, premium coverage)
   401(k) match percentage
   PTO policy (unlimited vs. defined days)
   Remote work flexibility
   Learning and development budget
   Equipment budget
   Risk level: NONE (but value varies)

EVALUATION FORMULA:
  Total Comp = Base + (Equity Value x Risk Factor) + Bonus

  Example:
  Base: $160K + Equity: $200K x 30% probability = $60K
  + Bonus: $16K (10% of base)
  = $236K total expected compensation

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │            TOTAL COMPENSATION DECISION TREE              │
  ├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │                                                         │
  │  Step 1: Calculate GUARANTEED comp                      │
  │  ├── Base salary                                        │

3. Salary Negotiation Scripts

Having specific language prepared for common salary negotiation moments removes the stress of improvisation. These scripts are starting points — adapt them to your voice and situation. The key is to practice them aloud until they feel natural, not rehearsed.

Scenario
/* ── Salary Negotiation Script Library ─────────────────────── */

SCRIPT 1: Responding to "What's your salary expectation?"
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "Based on my research of market rates for this role
  and level, and given the scope of what we've discussed,
  I'm targeting a total compensation package in the range
  of $175K to $195K. I'm flexible on how that breaks down
  between base, equity, and bonus — what matters most to
  me is the total value and the growth opportunity."

SCRIPT 2: Countering a low offer
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "Thank you for the offer — I'm excited about this role.
  I've done extensive market research, and for someone at
  my level with [specific experience], the market rate is
  $X to $Y. I'd love to find a way to close the gap.
  Would you be open to discussing [equity/signing bonus/
  title adjustment] as part of the package?"

SCRIPT 3: Negotiating equity
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "I'm very interested in the equity component. Could you
  help me understand: what's the total number of shares
  outstanding, what's the current 409A valuation, and
  what's the vesting schedule? I want to make sure I'm
  evaluating the full picture accurately."

SCRIPT 4: When they say the budget is fixed
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "I understand the base salary band is set. I'm wondering
  if there's flexibility on other components — perhaps a
  signing bonus, accelerated equity vesting, a performance
  review at 6 months instead of 12, or a commitment to a
  specific title/level path? I want to make this work."

SCRIPT 5: Asking for time to consider
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
  "This is an important decision and I want to give it
  the thought it deserves. Could I have until [specific
  date] to review everything and come back to you? I'm
  very interested in this opportunity."
  // ALWAYS ask for time. Never accept on the spot.
Common Mistake

Never disclose your current salary when asked. In many jurisdictions this question is now illegal, and in all cases it anchors the negotiation to your past rather than your market value. Redirect: "I'd prefer to focus on the market rate for this role and what I can contribute to this team."

4. Equity Negotiation for Startups

Equity is the most misunderstood component of startup compensation. Many candidates and early employees leave enormous value on the table because they don't know the right questions to ask. Founders, meanwhile, often give away too much equity early or structure it in ways that create problems later.

Understanding the mechanics of stock options, RSUs, vesting schedules, and dilution is not optional for anyone in the startup ecosystem. This is where the biggest financial outcomes are determined — and where the biggest mistakes are made.

Checklist
/* ── Equity Questions Every Startup Employee Must Ask ──────── */

BEFORE ACCEPTING EQUITY, GET ANSWERS TO:

Ownership Questions:
  [ ] How many shares am I being granted?
  [ ] What is the total number of fully diluted shares?
  [ ] What percentage does my grant represent?
  [ ] What is the current 409A (fair market value)?
  [ ] What was the last preferred share price (investors paid)?

Vesting Questions:
  [ ] What is the vesting schedule? (Standard: 4yr/1yr cliff)
  [ ] Is there acceleration on change of control (M&A)?
  [ ] Can I early exercise (83(b) election)?
  [ ] What happens to unvested shares if I'm terminated?

Dilution Questions:
  [ ] How much dilution should I expect at the next round?
  [ ] Is there an employee option pool refresh planned?
  [ ] What's the company's fundraising plan for next 18 months?

Exit Questions:
  [ ] What is the liquidation preference stack?
  [ ] In what exit scenario do common shares get paid?
  [ ] What's the exercise window if I leave? (90 days? 10 years?)
  [ ] Are there any secondary sale opportunities?

RED FLAGS:
  ✗ Company won't disclose total shares outstanding
  ✗ 90-day exercise window after departure
  ✗ No acceleration on change of control
  ✗ Preferred stock has > 1x liquidation preference
  ✗ No 409A valuation available
Tax Warning

The 83(b) election must be filed with the IRS within 30 days of receiving a stock grant. Missing this deadline is irreversible and can cost tens of thousands of dollars in unnecessary taxes. If you early-exercise stock options, consult a tax advisor immediately about filing an 83(b) election.

5. Timing and Leverage

When you negotiate matters almost as much as how you negotiate. Understanding the hiring cycle, budget timing, and leverage dynamics helps you choose the optimal moment to push for better terms.

Leverage in salary negotiation is not about power plays or threats. It is about having alternatives, demonstrating unique value, and choosing the right moment to have the conversation. The strongest position is genuine: you have options, you are excellent at what you do, and you are willing to walk away from a deal that does not reflect your value.

Framework
/* ── Timing Your Compensation Negotiation ──────────────────── */

BEST TIME TO NEGOTIATE (highest leverage):
  ✓ After you have a written offer (never before)
  ✓ When you have competing offers
  ✓ At the end of a fiscal quarter (hiring deadlines)
  ✓ When the team has urgent needs you uniquely fill
  ✓ After a successful trial project or contract period

WORST TIME TO NEGOTIATE (lowest leverage):
  ✗ Before they've decided they want you
  ✗ During mass layoffs in your industry
  ✗ When you have no alternatives
  ✗ Right after joining (wait for a review cycle)

LEVERAGE BOOSTERS:
  1. Multiple offers (even from companies you won't join)
  2. Specialized skills in high demand
  3. Strong referrals from people they respect
  4. Demonstrated results (portfolio, metrics, references)
  5. Willingness to walk away (the ultimate leverage)

FOR FOUNDERS HIRING:
  Be transparent about constraints and creative about solutions.
  "Our cash budget for this role is $140K, but I can offer
  0.5% equity with a 10-year exercise window and a guaranteed
  comp review in 6 months when we close our Series A."
  // Honesty + creativity beats pretending you have more budget

6. The Founder's Compensation Playbook

If you are building a startup, you will be on the other side of the table — designing compensation packages that attract great people while preserving runway and equity. This is one of the most consequential decisions you will make as a founder, and most get it wrong in predictable ways.

The most common founder mistakes are paying too much cash too early (burning runway), giving too much equity without structure (creating dilution problems), or building informal compensation systems that become unfair as the company grows. A thoughtful compensation philosophy established early pays dividends for years.

Framework
/* ── Founder's Compensation Design Framework ──────────────── */

STEP 1: DEFINE YOUR COMPENSATION PHILOSOPHY
  Choose your position on three spectrums:

  Cash vs. Equity:
  ├── High cash / low equity = competing with big companies
  └── Low cash / high equity = startup-native candidates

  Transparency:
  ├── Open salary bands = trust, fairness, less negotiation
  └── Individual negotiation = flexibility, potential unfairness

  Market positioning:
  ├── 75th percentile = attract top talent, higher burn
  ├── 50th percentile = market rate, sustainable
  └── 25th percentile = equity-heavy, mission-driven candidates

STEP 2: BUILD SALARY BANDS
  Level     | Base Range      | Equity Range | Total Target
  ──────────┼─────────────────┼──────────────┼─────────────
  Junior    | $90K - $120K    | 0.01 - 0.05% | $100 - $140K
  Mid       | $120K - $160K   | 0.05 - 0.15% | $150 - $200K
  Senior    | $150K - $200K   | 0.1  - 0.4%  | $200 - $300K
  Lead/Mgr  | $175K - $230K   | 0.2  - 0.75% | $250 - $400K
  Director+ | $200K - $280K   | 0.5  - 1.5%  | $350 - $600K

STEP 3: SCRIPT FOR TOUGH CONVERSATIONS
  When a candidate asks for more than your budget:
  "I want to be transparent — our cash budget for this
  role is $X. Here's what I can do to close the gap:
  [additional equity / signing bonus / accelerated review /
  title adjustment / flexibility benefits]. What matters
  most to you? Let's find a creative solution."

  // The best founders treat hiring negotiations as
  // the first collaboration with a future teammate.
Best Practice

Document every offer you make in a structured format with the same components. This creates consistency, reduces bias in compensation decisions, and protects you from accusations of unfair pay practices as your team grows.

Self-Check

Before moving to the next chapter, honestly answer these questions.

Can you calculate the value of a startup equity grant?

To value equity: (Number of shares / Fully diluted shares) x Latest valuation x Risk discount factor. If you can do this math and understand each variable, you can evaluate equity offers intelligently. Remember: the risk discount should be 50-80% for early-stage startups because most startups fail.

Do you have specific numbers for your market rate?

Not a range like '$130-180K' but a specific target like '$162K base + 0.15% equity' based on data from at least 3 sources. If your numbers are vague, your negotiation will be vague. Specificity signals preparation and commands respect.

Are you comfortable asking for time to review an offer?

This is a simple skill with enormous impact. Practice saying: 'I'm excited about this. Can I have until Friday to review the full package?' No employer who is worth working for will rescind an offer because you asked for 48 hours. If they do, that tells you everything you need to know about the company culture.

Can you name at least 5 components of total compensation beyond base salary?

Equity (stock options or RSUs), annual performance bonus, signing bonus, health insurance quality, 401(k) match, PTO policy, remote work flexibility, learning budget, equipment budget, title and level, review timeline, and severance terms. Each of these can be negotiated independently, and trading across them often unlocks value that a pure salary negotiation cannot.

Do you know the difference between ISOs and NSOs, and why it matters?

Incentive Stock Options (ISOs) receive favorable tax treatment if you hold the shares for at least 1 year after exercise and 2 years after grant. Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs) are taxed as ordinary income on exercise. ISOs are only available to employees; NSOs can be granted to contractors and advisors. The tax difference can be 15-20% of the option value, making this worth understanding before you negotiate.

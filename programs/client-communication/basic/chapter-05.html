Chapter 05 · Phase 3: Interpersonal

Giving and Receiving Feedback

Master the art of constructive feedback in client relationships. Learn frameworks for delivering difficult messages diplomatically and receiving criticism without defensiveness.

Estimated: 3–4 hours
3 Advancement Levels
3 Assignments

Why This Matters

Feedback is the mechanism that keeps projects on track. Without it, small misalignments compound into large failures. A client who does not give you honest feedback about a design mockup in week 2 becomes a client who is deeply unhappy with the finished product in week 12. A developer who cannot receive constructive criticism from a client becomes a developer who builds the wrong thing confidently.

In client services, feedback flows in both directions. You need to give feedback to clients about their requirements, their processes, and their decisions. Clients need to give feedback to you about your work, your communication, and your approach. Both directions require skill, and most professionals are bad at both.

Key Insight

The goal of feedback is not to be right. It is to improve outcomes. When you give feedback to a client, the measure of success is not whether they agree with you — it is whether the project outcome improves as a result of the conversation. This reframe changes everything about how you approach difficult feedback conversations.

Key Concepts

1. The SBI Feedback Model

The Situation-Behavior-Impact (SBI) model is the most effective framework for delivering constructive feedback. It removes judgment from the equation and focuses on observable facts and their consequences.

framework 
/* ── SBI FEEDBACK MODEL ────────────────────────── */

S — Situation: Describe the specific context
"During yesterday's sprint review meeting..."

B — Behavior: Describe the observable action
"...the requirements for the dashboard were changed
from what was agreed in the spec document..."

I — Impact: Describe the effect on the project
"...which means we need to re-do approximately 3 days
of development work and will likely push the delivery
date by one week."

R — Request (optional): What you need going forward
"Going forward, could we agree that spec changes go
through a change request process so we can assess
the impact before work begins?"

2. Types of Client Feedback Scenarios

Different feedback scenarios require different approaches. Here are the most common situations and how to handle each:

template 
/* ── FEEDBACK SCENARIO TEMPLATES ───────────────── */

Scenario: Client's requirements are unclear
"I want to make sure we build exactly what you need.
The current requirement for [feature] could be
interpreted in two ways: [interpretation A] or
[interpretation B]. Could we schedule 15 minutes
to align on which direction is correct? This will
save us from having to rebuild later."

Scenario: Client's timeline is unrealistic
"I appreciate the urgency around the [date] deadline.
After analyzing the scope, our estimate is [X weeks].
Here are three options:
1. Reduce scope to [core features] — meets deadline
2. Add [N] developers — meets deadline at [cost]
3. Keep full scope — delivery moves to [date]
Which option works best for your priorities?"

Scenario: Client keeps changing requirements
"I have noticed that the requirements for [feature]
have changed [N] times in the past [timeframe]. Each
change has added approximately [X days] to the timeline.
I want to make sure we are building the right thing.
Could we implement a brief change request process
to evaluate impact before we redirect the team?"

Scenario: Positive feedback / recognition
"I wanted to acknowledge that the design specs your
team provided for [feature] were exceptionally clear.
Having detailed wireframes and acceptance criteria
saved us approximately [X hours] of back-and-forth.
If all features could follow this format, it would
significantly improve our velocity."
Critical Rule

Never give negative feedback to a client in a group setting unless absolutely necessary. Difficult feedback should be delivered one-on-one with the primary stakeholder first, then discussed in a group only if the stakeholder agrees it is appropriate. Public criticism damages relationships in ways that are very difficult to repair.

3. Receiving Client Feedback Gracefully

When a client gives you critical feedback about your work, your instinct will be to defend yourself. Resist that instinct. The HEAR framework helps you respond constructively:

framework 
/* ── HEAR: Receiving Feedback Framework ────────── */

H — Hear: Listen fully without interrupting
Let the client finish their entire thought before
you respond. Do not formulate your defense while
they are speaking.

E — Empathize: Acknowledge their perspective
"I understand why that is frustrating. If I were in
your position, I would feel the same way."

A — Ask: Clarify to ensure understanding
"Can you help me understand what specifically about
the [feature] did not meet your expectations? I want
to make sure I address the right issue."

R — Respond: Share your plan to address it
"Thank you for that feedback. Here is what I will do:
[specific action] by [specific date]. I will follow
up with you on [date] to make sure it meets your
expectations."

4. Giving Feedback on Client Design and Requirements

Sometimes the client's own work — their designs, their requirements, their business logic — needs improvement. This is the most delicate feedback scenario because you are critiquing the client's expertise in their own domain.

template 
/* ── DESIGN REVIEW FEEDBACK TEMPLATE ──────────── */

Subject: [Project] Design Review — [Feature Name]

Hi [Client Name],

Thank you for sharing the designs for [feature]. The
overall direction is strong, and the user flow for
[specific element] is particularly well thought out.

I have a few observations from a technical and UX
perspective that I would like to discuss:

1. [Observation]
   Current: [What the design shows]
   Consideration: [Technical or UX concern]
   Suggestion: [Alternative approach]
   Impact: [Why this matters for users/performance]

2. [Observation]
   Current: [What the design shows]
   Consideration: [Technical or UX concern]
   Suggestion: [Alternative approach]
   Impact: [Why this matters for users/performance]

These are suggestions, not requirements. You know your
users best. I wanted to surface these considerations
so we can make an informed decision together.

Would a 15-minute call this week work to discuss?

Best,
[Your name]

How to handle feedback you disagree with

Not all feedback is valid, but all feedback is data. When a client gives you feedback you believe is incorrect, do not dismiss it. Instead, ask clarifying questions to understand the underlying concern. Often the specific feedback is wrong but it points to a real issue. For example, "the app is too slow" might actually mean "I cannot find what I need quickly enough" — which is a navigation problem, not a performance problem.

What if the client never gives feedback?

Silence is not approval. If a client is not giving feedback, it usually means one of three things: they are too busy, they do not feel safe being critical, or they have disengaged. Actively solicit feedback with specific questions: "On a scale of 1-10, how satisfied are you with our communication this sprint? What would make it a 10?"

Assignments

Complete these in order. Use real feedback situations whenever possible.

Assignment 1
Level 1 — Foundation

Practice the SBI Model

Identify three real situations where you need to give feedback and write out the SBI script for each.

  • Write 3 SBI feedback scripts (one positive, one constructive, one about a process issue)
  • Practice delivering each script out loud to a peer or mirror
  • Time each delivery — target 60-90 seconds per feedback item
  • Get peer review on whether the feedback is specific, factual, and actionable
  • Deliver at least one of the three in a real conversation
Done when

A peer confirms that all three scripts are specific, non-judgmental, and include actionable next steps.

Assignment 2
Level 2 — Professional

Conduct a Feedback Exchange

Have a structured feedback conversation with a client or colleague using both giving and receiving frameworks.

  • Give feedback using the SBI model on a real project issue
  • Receive feedback using the HEAR framework (ask a client for honest feedback on your communication)
  • Document both conversations: what you said, how they responded, what you would change
  • Follow up within 48 hours on any commitments made during the feedback exchange
Done when

Both parties report the conversation was productive, and at least one concrete improvement is implemented.

Assignment 3
Level 3 — Production-Ready

Implement a Client Feedback System

Design and deploy a systematic feedback collection process for your client engagement.

  • Create a sprint-end feedback form (3-5 questions, takes under 2 minutes to complete)
  • Design a quarterly satisfaction survey with NPS and open-ended questions
  • Define how feedback will be reviewed, categorized, and acted upon
  • Run the system for at least 2 sprints and document what you learned
  • Present feedback insights and process improvements to your team
Done when

The feedback system is running, at least 80% of clients respond, and you can point to specific improvements driven by feedback data.

Self-Check

Before moving to Chapter 06, assess yourself.

Can you deliver constructive feedback without triggering defensiveness?

The test is not whether you felt the feedback was well-delivered. The test is whether the recipient was able to hear it and act on it. Ask for their reaction. If they became defensive, analyze what triggered it — was it your wording, your tone, the setting, or the timing?

Can you receive criticism without becoming defensive?

Record yourself in a feedback conversation (with permission). Watch for defensive body language: crossed arms, interrupting, explaining before understanding, or deflecting to someone else. The HEAR framework only works if you genuinely listen before responding.

Do you actively solicit feedback or just wait for it?

Count how many times in the last month you proactively asked a client for feedback on your work or communication. If the answer is zero, start with one simple question at the end of your next meeting: "Is there anything we could do differently to make these meetings more useful for you?"

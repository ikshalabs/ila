Chapter 03 · Communication Foundations

Giving and Receiving Feedback

Build a feedback culture that accelerates growth instead of creating resentment. In startups where iteration speed is everything, the ability to give clear, actionable feedback — and to receive it without defensiveness — is a competitive advantage.

Estimated: 3–4 hours
5 Key Concepts
3 Assignments

Why This Matters

In a startup environment, the feedback loop between team members, between teams and clients, and between managers and reports is the engine of improvement. When feedback flows freely and constructively, teams iterate faster, catch mistakes earlier, and build stronger relationships with clients. When feedback is absent, vague, or delivered poorly, small problems compound into major crises.

Research from Gallup shows that employees who receive regular, meaningful feedback are 3.6 times more likely to be engaged at work. In a startup where retention and morale directly impact your ability to ship, this is not a soft skill — it is an operational imperative.

The challenge is that most people have never been taught how to give feedback well. They either avoid it entirely (leading to festering issues) or deliver it so bluntly that it damages the relationship. This chapter teaches you structured frameworks that make feedback specific, actionable, and safe to give and receive.

Key Insight

Feedback is not criticism. Criticism says "this is wrong." Feedback says "here is what I observed, here is the impact, and here is what I suggest." The former triggers defensiveness. The latter opens dialogue. Master the difference and you will become the person everyone wants to work with.

Key Concepts

1. The SBI Feedback Model

The Situation-Behavior-Impact (SBI) model, developed by the Center for Creative Leadership, is the most reliable framework for delivering clear, non-judgmental feedback. It works because it separates observable facts from personal interpretation.

 ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
 │              SBI FEEDBACK MODEL                  │
 │                                                  │
 │  S — SITUATION                                   │
 │      When and where did this happen?             │
 │      "During yesterday's client demo..."         │
 │                                                  │
 │  B — BEHAVIOR                                    │
 │      What specifically did the person do?         │
 │      "...you interrupted the client twice         │
 │       while they were describing their needs..."  │
 │                                                  │
 │  I — IMPACT                                      │
 │      What was the effect of that behavior?        │
 │      "...which made them visibly frustrated       │
 │       and they stopped sharing context."          │
 │                                                  │
 │  Optional: REQUEST                                │
 │      What would you like to see instead?          │
 │      "Next time, could you note your thoughts     │
 │       and wait for a natural pause?"              │
 └─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
Figure 1: The SBI Feedback Model with optional Request extension
SBI EXAMPLES
POSITIVE FEEDBACK (reinforcing):

Situation: "In this morning's standup..."
Behavior: "...you flagged the API dependency blocker
           before it affected anyone else's work..."
Impact:   "...which saved Marcus at least half a day
           and kept us on track for the sprint goal."

CONSTRUCTIVE FEEDBACK (redirecting):

Situation: "During the client email thread yesterday..."
Behavior: "...you committed to a Friday delivery date
           without checking with the team first..."
Impact:   "...which put us in a tough spot because QA
           needs at least 2 days, and we now risk missing
           a deadline we cannot control."
Request:  "In the future, could you reply with 'Let me
           confirm timing with the team' before committing
           to specific dates externally?"

/* Notice: no judgment words like "careless" or "bad"
   — only observable facts and their consequences. */

2. Radical Candor Framework

Kim Scott's Radical Candor framework maps feedback styles on two axes: Care Personally (do you genuinely care about the person?) and Challenge Directly (are you willing to be honest?). The intersection creates four quadrants:

                    CHALLENGE DIRECTLY
                          ▲
                          │
         Obnoxious        │        Radical
         Aggression       │        Candor
         "You're bad at   │  "I care about your growth,
          presentations"  │   and I noticed X. Here's
                          │   how we can fix it."
  ────────────────────────┼────────────────────────►
                          │                CARE
         Manipulative     │        Ruinous     PERSONALLY
         Insincerity      │        Empathy
         "Nice job"       │  "It was fine..."
         (behind back:    │   (thinks: it was terrible
          "that was       │    but doesn't want to
           terrible")     │    hurt feelings)
                          │
Figure 2: Radical Candor's four quadrants

The goal is the upper-right quadrant: Radical Candor. You care enough about the person to tell them the truth, and you respect them enough to be direct. The most common failure mode in startups is Ruinous Empathy — being so concerned about hurting feelings that you withhold the feedback people need to grow.

Watch Out

Radical Candor is not a license to be harsh. The "Care Personally" axis is not optional — it is the foundation. If someone does not believe you genuinely care about their success, your direct feedback will always land as aggression, regardless of how carefully you word it. Build the relationship first, then use it as the vehicle for honest feedback.

3. Structuring Effective 1:1 Conversations

The weekly 1:1 is the most underutilized tool for feedback in most startups. When done well, it is the safe space where feedback flows both directions — manager to report and report to manager. When done poorly, it is a status update that could have been an email.

1:1 MEETING TEMPLATE
1:1 AGENDA — [Manager] + [Report]
Duration: 30 minutes | Frequency: Weekly

[5 min] Check-In
- How are you doing? (genuinely, not as small talk)
- Anything on your mind before we start?

[10 min] Their Agenda
- What is top of mind for you this week?
- Any blockers I can help remove?
- Any feedback for me on how I can support you better?

[10 min] Your Agenda
- SBI feedback on specific recent situations
- Coaching on upcoming challenges
- Career development check-in (monthly)

[5 min] Commitments
- What are we each committing to before next 1:1?
- Any follow-ups needed?

/* KEY RULE: The report's agenda comes first.
   This is THEIR meeting, not yours. If they have
   nothing to discuss, that itself is data — either
   they do not feel safe, or you are not creating
   enough space for them to surface issues. */

4. Receiving Feedback Without Defensiveness

Giving feedback is hard. Receiving it well is even harder. Your instinctive reaction to critical feedback is to defend, deflect, or shut down. These responses are natural — your brain perceives feedback as a threat. The skill is learning to override that instinct with a deliberate process.

The HEAR model for receiving feedback:

  • H — Halt. Pause before responding. Take a breath. Do not immediately explain or defend.
  • E — Empathize. Acknowledge that the other person took a risk by giving you this feedback. Thank them for it, even if it stings.
  • A — Ask. Clarify with questions: "Can you give me a specific example?" or "What would good look like in that situation?"
  • R — Reflect. Take time to process before committing to action. Say: "Thank you for this. I want to think about it and follow up with you tomorrow."
RECEIVING FEEDBACK — SCRIPTS
/* When you receive constructive feedback: */

DEFENSIVE (natural but unhelpful):
"That's not what happened. The client was already
 frustrated before I said anything."

PRODUCTIVE (deliberate and growth-oriented):
"Thank you for telling me that. I appreciate you
 being direct. Can you give me a specific example
 of the moment where I lost the room? I want to
 understand so I can handle it better next time."

/* When you disagree with feedback: */

DISMISSIVE:
"I disagree. I think it went fine."

EXPLORATORY:
"I hear you, and I had a different read on the
 situation. Can we walk through what happened?
 I want to understand your perspective because
 I may be missing something."

/* When you need time to process: */

"This is really valuable feedback. I want to sit
 with it rather than react in the moment. Can we
 revisit this in our 1:1 on Thursday? I want to
 come back with a thoughtful response."

5. Building a Feedback Culture on Your Team

Individual feedback skills are necessary but not sufficient. For feedback to work at scale, it needs to be embedded in your team's culture — normalized, expected, and safe. Here is how to build that culture:

  • Model it publicly. Ask for feedback on yourself in team settings: "What is one thing I could do better in these meetings?" When the leader goes first, it signals safety.
  • Make it frequent and small. Feedback should not be a quarterly event tied to performance reviews. It should be a daily occurrence — quick, specific, and in the moment.
  • Separate feedback from evaluation. Coaching conversations (1:1s) should feel different from performance reviews. If people fear that every piece of feedback will appear in their review, they will stop being honest.
  • Create structured rituals. Sprint retrospectives, peer code reviews, and design critiques are all built-in feedback mechanisms. Use them intentionally.
Pro Tip

Start with positive feedback. Most people have a 5:1 ratio expectation — they need to hear 5 pieces of positive feedback for every 1 piece of constructive feedback to feel psychologically safe. If you have never given someone positive feedback and then hit them with something critical, it will land badly regardless of your technique.

How to give feedback to someone more senior than you

Giving upward feedback is intimidating but essential. Use the same SBI framework, but lead with the impact on you or the team: "When the decision to change the API architecture was made without the team's input [Situation], it was announced via email with no discussion [Behavior], and the team felt excluded from a decision that directly affects our daily work [Impact]. Could we discuss having a brief sync before major architectural changes?" Frame it as a shared interest in better outcomes, not a complaint.

How to handle feedback you fundamentally disagree with

Not all feedback is accurate. Sometimes the person giving feedback has incomplete information or different values. The key is to separate the signal from the noise. Ask yourself: (1) Is there any truth here, even if the framing is off? (2) Have I heard similar feedback from others? (3) If I imagine my most respected colleague watching the situation, would they agree? If the answer to all three is genuinely no, acknowledge the perspective ("I hear you, thank you for sharing that") and do not feel obligated to change.

The foundational framework for separating observation from judgment in all communication.

Assignments

Complete these assignments in order. Practice with real situations whenever possible.

Assignment 1
Level 1 — Foundation

SBI Practice with Real Scenarios

Write SBI-formatted feedback for 5 real situations from your recent work experience.

  • Write 3 positive SBI feedback examples from the past 2 weeks
  • Write 2 constructive SBI feedback examples with a Request component
  • Review each for judgment words and replace with observable behaviors
  • Actually deliver at least 2 of these feedback pieces this week
  • Document how the recipient responded and what you would adjust
Done when

You have delivered real SBI feedback to at least 2 people and documented the experience, including what went well and what you would change.

Assignment 2
Level 2 — Professional

Design Your 1:1 Framework

Create a structured 1:1 framework and use it for at least 4 consecutive weeks.

  • Create a 1:1 template based on the framework in this chapter
  • Share it with your manager or report and agree on the format
  • Conduct 4 consecutive weekly 1:1s using the template
  • Include at least one piece of SBI feedback in each session
  • After 4 weeks, ask for feedback on the 1:1 format itself and iterate
Done when

You have 4 weeks of documented 1:1 notes showing a consistent structure, feedback exchange, and iterative improvement of the format itself.

Assignment 3
Level 3 — Leadership

Launch a Team Feedback Ritual

Design and implement a team-wide feedback mechanism that runs for at least one full sprint cycle.

  • Train your team on the SBI model in a 15-minute session
  • Introduce a "feedback round" in your retrospective format
  • Model vulnerability by asking for feedback on yourself publicly
  • Run the ritual for 2 sprint cycles and collect team sentiment
  • Document what worked, what did not, and your recommendations for scaling it
Done when

Your team has participated in at least 2 sprint cycles of the feedback ritual, and team members report feeling more comfortable giving and receiving feedback.

Self-Check

Before moving to Chapter 04, honestly answer these questions.

Can you deliver constructive feedback using SBI without judgment language?

Write an SBI statement right now for a recent situation. Read it aloud. Does it contain any words like "always," "never," "careless," or "wrong"? If so, rewrite it with observable behaviors only. The test is whether the recipient could watch a video of the situation and agree with your description of what happened.

Do you have a process for receiving feedback without becoming defensive?

Think about the last time someone gave you constructive feedback. Did you thank them, ask clarifying questions, and take time to reflect? Or did you immediately explain, justify, or deflect? If the latter, practice the HEAR model with a trusted colleague who can give you feedback and hold you accountable for the response pattern.

Is feedback a regular occurrence in your team, or a rare event?

Count the number of times you gave or received specific, actionable feedback in the past week — not in a formal review, but in the normal flow of work. If the number is less than 3, feedback is not yet a habit on your team. Start with one piece of positive SBI feedback per day and build from there.
